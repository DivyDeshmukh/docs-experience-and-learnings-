## The Sheet Question — Which One

Use **NeetCode 150** as your primary sheet. Not Striver's 450, not Love Babbar 450, not a random 1000-problem list. Here's why NeetCode 150 wins for your situation:

It's already organized by pattern, not by difficulty. Every problem is handpicked — no filler. It covers exactly the patterns we mapped in your sprint plan. And NeetCode has free video explanations for every single problem in case you get completely stuck. For someone running a tight daily schedule alongside a job and GATE prep, this is the right density.

The 150 problems are not the destination. They are the curriculum. The practice comes after, and that's the part most people miss.

## The Exact Daily Approach

Here's how a single DSA session should actually run — not just "do 2 problems."

**Step 1 — Attempt blind (20–25 minutes max)**
Open the problem. Read it twice. Don't look at any hints, solutions, or discussions. Try to solve it. If you solve it, great — move to Step 2. If you don't solve it within 25 minutes, stop trying and move to Step 2 anyway. The attempt is not wasted even if you fail. The attempt is actually the most important part — it loads the problem into your brain in a way that makes the solution stick permanently.

**Step 2 — If you solved it: review and optimize**
Does your solution pass all test cases? What's the time and space complexity? Can you do better? Look at the NeetCode video or top solutions — not to copy, but to see if there's a cleaner approach. Sometimes your O(n²) solution has an O(n) version using a hashmap you didn't think of. Understanding that gap is the actual learning.

**If you didn't solve it: read the approach only, then implement**
This is critical. Don't watch the full video and then code along. Read or watch just enough to understand the core insight — the "aha moment" of how to approach it. Then close the solution. Then implement it yourself from scratch with that insight in your head. This process — attempt → understand approach → implement without looking — is what builds real pattern recognition. Watching and typing along builds nothing.

**Step 3 — Write one line in your error log**
"Two Sum — missed that hashmap stores complement, not the number itself." One line. This takes 20 seconds and will save you hours in revision later.

**Step 4 — Spaced repetition on Day 3 and Day 7**
Come back to problems you struggled with. Not all problems — just the ones where you needed to look at the approach. Reattempt them cold. If you can solve it cleanly now, it's locked in. If you still struggle, it goes back in the queue.

## The Practice Layer — After the Sheet

This is what you asked about and what most guides skip.

Once you finish a pattern in NeetCode 150, you don't move on immediately. You spend 2–3 days doing **unseen problems in the same pattern** before moving to the next pattern. This is how pattern recognition actually gets built — not by doing 5 hashmap problems and calling it done, but by doing 5, then seeing 3 more you've never seen and solving them using the same intuition.

**Where to get practice problems by pattern:**

For each pattern, after NeetCode 150, go to LeetCode → Topics filter → select the relevant tag → sort by Acceptance Rate descending → do 3–5 mediums you haven't seen. That's it. You don't need another sheet. You need unseen problems in a controlled pattern.

The specific practice flow per pattern:

After finishing Two Pointers in NeetCode → do 3 unseen Two Pointer mediums on LeetCode cold → only then move to Sliding Window.

After finishing Trees → do 4–5 unseen Tree mediums → move to Graphs.

After finishing DP (this one needs more practice) → do 5–6 unseen DP mediums, specifically targeting the sub-pattern you found hardest.

## The Weekly Contest — Non-Negotiable From Day 1

Every Sunday, do the LeetCode Weekly Contest. Don't wait until you feel ready. Start this Sunday.

The first few contests will feel humbling. You might solve 1 out of 4. That's completely fine. The contest does something the sheet cannot — it puts you under time pressure with problems you've never seen, which is exactly the condition of a real interview. The discomfort of a contest is training you can't get any other way.

After every contest, whether you did well or terribly, spend 30 minutes upsolving the problems you couldn't finish. Read the approach → implement cold. This is the single highest-ROI activity in all of DSA preparation.

## The Mental Model to Carry

Think of NeetCode 150 as your textbook and LeetCode unseen problems as your exam practice. You need both. The textbook teaches you the concept. The exam tests whether you actually learned it or just memorized the example.

Most people do only the textbook and wonder why they freeze in interviews. Some people jump straight to random practice and wonder why nothing sticks. You do both, in sequence, per pattern.

Pattern → NeetCode problems → unseen practice → contest → move to next pattern.

That loop, repeated 8 times across your sprint, is what takes you from where you are today to the person who walks into an interview and recognizes problems instead of panicking at them.
